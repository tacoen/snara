
### Chapter 9

#### Beat 32 – The Shadow

The new sugar mill stood gleaming under the blazing Semarang sun, red banners snapping against whitewashed walls. Peter Chang—Ta Laupan himself—stood on the raised platform, voice rolling across the gathered workers and guests like distant thunder. "Without Awei, none of this would run so smoothly," he declared, clapping Wei on the shoulder with firm pride. "A great manager and a devoted son-in-law. The family name is in safe hands."

Wei smiled, chest swelling for a moment beneath the public praise. The workers clapped, the sun pressed down, and the thick sweetness of fresh molasses hung heavy in the air. For one brief instant he felt the old satisfaction of being seen as the perfect heir.

But then his gaze slid sideways. Sophia stood only a few paces away, close enough that her white dress brushed Charles van Dijk’s sleeve. The Dutch doctor leaned in, murmuring something in soft Dutch. Sophia laughed, head tilting, her hand resting lightly on his arm as if it belonged there. The sound of their shared language cut through the applause like a private joke Wei was not invited to join. The praise in his ears turned sour, curdling into something sharp and metallic.

Later, in the shade of the factory veranda, the air carried the faint asem of cooled sweat and the distant clatter of lorries. Wei watched Peter Chang speak quietly with Mbok Yati near the carved teak railing. The old maid stood with her usual straight back, yet something in the way she held herself felt softer, almost familiar. Peter’s hand rested on her shoulder a moment longer than necessary, thumb brushing the edge of her kebaya in a slow, absent stroke. Mbok Yati did not pull away. Instead her wrinkled face tilted up toward him, a small, knowing smile curving her lips—the same smile Wei had seen her give only when counting extra notes in the kitchen. Peter’s voice dropped even lower, the words lost to the breeze, but the tone carried the easy warmth of long habit, the kind of warmth that came from years of shared silences and closed doors.

To anyone else it would have looked perfectly proper: the old master consulting his most trusted babu about household arrangements. Sophia, standing a little farther along the veranda with Little Peter on her hip, glanced over and offered a gentle nod of approval, as if nothing could be more natural than her father’s quiet conversation with the woman who had served the family since before Sophia could walk. She adjusted the baby’s sun hat and turned back to Charles without a second thought, her laugh rising again at whatever the Dutchman had just said.

Wei’s jaw tightened. The gesture between Peter and Mbok Yati lingered in his mind—the brief press of fingers, the way her shoulder leaned ever so slightly into the touch before both of them straightened, faces once more composed and dutiful. Bagas stepped closer, voice low beside Wei’s ear.

"Mbok Yati has old rumors, Tuan. People say she was more than just a maid to Ta Laupan. That she slept with the master himself." Bagas’s eyes flicked toward the pair. "Witnessing their closeness now... I’m sure it was not just an old rumor. One of Semarang’s quiet secrets."

Wei gave a short, disbelieving laugh. "You really believe that? So you’re saying they were already fucking before the Japanese came? Before Bersiap?"

Bagas kept his voice steady. "Of course. She was already working here long before Nona Sophia was born. When Bersiap happened, Ta Laupan fled to Australia with his wife. They never told you that?"

Wei shook his head. "I only knew her in 1949."

Bagas leaned in a fraction closer. "Then you were not aware that Peter returned to Indonesia for her. She was his concubine."

Wei looked momentarily confused, the pieces shifting in his mind. "That explains why I only saw my mother-in-law when I married Sophia."

Bagas gave a small, dry chuckle. "You are inheriting more than the sugar business, Tuan Muda. I am sure Ta Laupan did not only fuck her. Just like you."

The words landed like cold water down Wei’s spine. But now he saw the clear motive behind Peter Chang’s long silences and quiet returns. His wife had been away in Sydney, and this intimacy had waited for him here all along. Mbok Yati might expose everything to Peter Chang—the babu, the shaman herbs, the new cock, the secrets that could destroy the perfect image Peter had just praised in front of everyone. Wei’s jaw tightened. He turned to Bagas, voice low and hard. "Act fast. Find someone who can handle this quietly."

Later he tapped Bagas on the arm. "Act really fast. We also have to deal with the unbalanced ledgers next."

Bagas nodded once, agreement sharp in his eyes.

The next day Bagas returned with a man he introduced as Cak Ireng. The stranger was menacing, with an intimidating mustache and eyes that never softened. He wore a black klambi over a white-and-red striped shirt, a short gook tucked visibly in his belt. The introduction was easy—Wei from Surabaya, Cak Ireng from Madura—both men slipping into the rough Suroboyo Javanese slang that felt like home.

Cak Ireng bowed slightly, voice gravelly. "Tuan Muda, konco cino."

Wei brought him into the small gang with money and the promise of a secret hideout in the sugar mill. Cak Ireng accepted with a single nod. In the dim back office of the old mill, away from prying eyes, Wei stated his problem plainly. He needed Mbok Yati silenced before she could speak to Peter Chang.

From that night on, Wei, Bagas, and Cak Ireng met secretly in the sugar mill office, the air thick with cigarette smoke and the distant hum of night insects. The plan to remove the old maid grew darker with every whispered word.

Bagas had overheard Sophia planning for the weekend with his father: a short family trip to the Bandungan villa and Gedong Songo temple. Wei listened, brow furrowed. He knew this trip was not for Peter, but to entertain Charles. The realization escalated quickly, expanding the plan to eliminate Charles too. Cak Ireng burst with anger at the mention of the Dutchman, but the meeting ended with no action yet taken.

The next morning Wei asked Sophia about the plan. He was surprised to learn he had been the last to know. Sophia offered a soft apology, touching his wrist with the same gentle warmth she always showed. Wei hid his jealousy well, smiling as if nothing troubled him. When he once again saw how Peter interacted with Mbok Yati—another quiet word, another lingering hand on her shoulder—an idea sprang to life. He saw the perfect opportunity and suggested that Mbok Yati go one day early "to prepare everything so Cici can rest, and Papa will be happy."

"Fine!" Sophia answered. "But I plan to go with Charles there directly from the hospital, after the night shift."

Wei saw an even more perfect opening. "Sure, it is easier for you. Fine. Papa and I will go there in the morning."

So the plan formed. Bagas was ordered to drive Mbok Yati before Sophia and Charles arrived, and Cak Ireng would travel separately to wait hidden at the villa. On Sunday morning, after church, Wei and Peter would arrive. "When I arrive with Ta Laupan," Wei told Bagas and Cak Ireng, "everything must be done."

#### Beat 34 – The Villa

Friday noon, after the midday prayer, Bagas pulled the car up to the Chang family villa in Bandungan. The highland air carried the sharp scent of pine and damp earth, a cool contrast to the city’s sticky heat. Mbok Yati stepped out with her small bag, back straight as always, kebaya crisp despite the journey. In her mind the place stirred sweet-bitter memories—the quiet nights when Tuan Besar Peter had once drawn her close in the same shadowed rooms, his hands familiar on her skin, long before the Japanese came or the Bersiap tore everything apart. She expected nothing more than the usual work: airing linens, preparing meals, making the villa ready for the family. She had no idea Cak Ireng was already hidden in the trees behind the compound, waiting as Wei had ordered.

The old maid greeted Sophia and Charles when they arrived late that night from the hospital. "Non Sophia, Tuan Charles," she said, voice warm with practiced respect. "You two look good together. Like a perfect couple."

Cak Ireng, crouched low in the undergrowth, heard every word. The praise curdled in his gut. "Dasar penghianat," he muttered under his breath. "Kon pantas mati." He had fought men like Charles during the revolution; now he watched this one laugh softly with Sophia, their hands brushing too easily. When the pair slipped into the master bathroom together, the sound of running water followed by low moans drifting through the open window, his hatred hardened. He slipped inside later, silent as smoke, and took Charles’s belt, socks, and watch—small things to plant later.

Saturday morning Sophia and Charles left early for Gedong Songo, the ancient temples rising cool and misty on the slopes of Mount Ungaran. Mbok Yati stayed behind to tend the villa. Cak Ireng knew the moment had come.

He moved from the trees like a shadow, grabbed the old woman from behind in the garden path, and dragged her into the small warehouse at the back of the compound. The air inside smelled of dust and stored rice sacks. Mbok Yati tried to fight, clawing at his arms, but Cak Ireng was far stronger. He pinned her down on the rough wooden floor, his breath hot against her ear.

"Penghianat!" he growled. "Antek londo! Ngentot antek Belanda!"

Mbok Yati gasped, eyes wide with terror. "Tolong... jangan... saya cuma babu..."

"Kamu siapa? Kenapa jahat?" she managed, voice cracking as she tried to twist away. It was too late.

He ignored her broken pleas. He forced himself on her repeatedly, his grip rough on her frail frame, tearing at her kebaya until the fabric gave way. Each thrust carried the full weight of his hatred—for the Dutch, for traitors, for the old order that still lingered in houses like this. Her struggles weakened, her voice fading to nothing more than soft, defeated whimpers.

"Tolong... jangan... jangan..."

"Jangan?" he cursed, eyes blazing. "Kamu penghianat! Mati kon! Mati!"

He wrapped his hands around her throat and squeezed until the life left her. 

When it was done he scattered Charles’s stolen belongings around the body, arranged the scene to look like a desperate struggle, and locked the warehouse door from the outside. 

He slipped back into the main house, found Charles’s leather bag by the bed, and tucked the key inside. Everything exactly as Wei had planned. Then Cak Ireng vanished down the hill path, the pine-scented wind swallowing his footsteps.

He knew the prize was waiting for him.

#### Beat 35 – The Lovers

Sophia and Charles returned late that afternoon, flushed from the temple walk and the long drive. The villa felt empty, but they were too caught in each other to notice. Sophia’s voice dropped low and urgent as she pulled Charles toward the bedroom. "Charles... neuk me. Neuk me nog een keer, voordat mijn stomme man komt."

They fell onto the bed together, clothes shed in hurried layers. The late sunlight slanted through the half-drawn curtains, warming their skin as they moved with the easy hunger of stolen time. 

Sophia’s hands traced the lines of his back, her breath catching in soft Dutch gasps that grew sharper, more desperate. Charles answered with quiet intensity, the bed creaking beneath them in steady rhythm. 

For those brief hours the outside world—the husband, the father, the waiting secrets—faded completely. They lost themselves in each other until the room grew dim and the first evening chill crept through the open window.

#### Beat 36 – The Discovery

Sunday morning broke clear and cool. Wei arrived with Peter Chang just after church, the car wheels crunching on the gravel drive. Peter stepped out first, calling cheerfully, "Yati? Mbok Yati?" The name echoed through the quiet villa. No answer came.

Sophia appeared from the guest room, hair still damp from a quick wash, smiling as she greeted them. "Papa, Koko. You’re early." She acted perfectly composed, as if she and Charles had slept in separate rooms all night. "Charles is still resting. Let me call him."

When Charles joined them, rubbing sleep from his eyes, Sophia asked lightly, "Have you seen Mbok Yati? She should have prepared breakfast by now."

Charles shook his head. "I haven’t seen her since last night."

Wei kept his face carefully neutral and walked toward the master bedroom, calling the old maid’s name in unison with the others. In the bathroom he paused, then bent and picked up a pair of Sophia’s lace underwear left carelessly on the tile floor. He slipped it into his pocket without a word.

They searched the kitchen next, then the small maid’s room—her belongings neatly folded, but the bed untouched. Wei led them outside to the locked warehouse door at the back of the compound. "Where is the key?" he asked, voice mild, eyes fixed on Charles.

Charles looked puzzled. "I don’t know."

Wei turned back toward the house. "Perhaps it’s in your bag. I’ll check." He returned moments later holding the key, making it sound like simple coincidence. "Found it inside your things. Strange place for it."

Sophia opened her mouth to protest, but Wei stepped close and pressed the lace underwear into her hand, whispering low enough that only she could hear, "Don’t let Papa see this." His eyes held hers a beat longer—clear, cold knowledge that she had been with Charles. She closed her fingers around the fabric, cheeks paling, and said nothing.

Bagas arrived then, stepping out of the second car with a questioning look. "What happened?"

Peter’s face had tightened with worry. "Yati is missing."

Wei handed the key to Bagas exactly as planned. "Open it."

Bagas turned the lock. The door swung open on heavy hinges. Inside, Mbok Yati’s body lay crumpled among the scattered belongings—Charles’s belt, socks, and watch glinting in the dim light. Peter staggered forward, a raw cry breaking from his throat as he dropped to his knees beside her. Tears cut through the old man’s face; the sight of his longtime lover, the woman who had shared his secrets for decades, shattered something deep inside him.

Sophia and Charles stood frozen, shock whitening their faces. Wei and Bagas played their parts perfectly—expressions of horror, voices low with concern.

Bagas spoke first, voice steady. "When I drove her here on Friday, she told me Charles had threatened her. Said he wanted to avenge what happened to his people during Bersiap. She was frightened, Tuan."

All eyes turned to Charles. The Dutchman shook his head, voice rising in disbelief. "No—that’s a lie. Sophia, tell them. You know I never—"

Sophia could not speak. She stood rigid, the weight of what she and Charles had done last night pressing on her throat. Not in front of her mourning father. Not in front of her husband. The truth of her affair would destroy everything. She could only stare at the ground, silent, while the morning sun climbed higher and the pine-scented wind carried the first faint stirrings of accusation through the villa.

Peter’s voice rose suddenly, high and trembling with fury. "Londo jahat! Kurang puas kah kalian?"

The words cracked through the quiet like a whip. Wei had never heard Peter speak like that—raw, venomous, the old colonial wound splitting open. 

He glanced at Bagas, startled. Bagas moved quickly to Peter’s side, voice low and respectful. "Kita kubur dia di belakang, di bawah pohon pinus, Tuan Besar?"

Wei looked to Sophia. She nodded quickly, eyes downcast, agreeing without a word.

Charles still protested, voice cracking. "No! I didn’t do it! Please believe me!"

But he knew Sophia could not speak for him. And Wei, standing silently beside them, made no move to help.

Inside him, a victorious grin spread. He had hit two birds with one stone.

The morning light slanted through the pine branches, turning the warehouse doorway into a sharp rectangle of accusation while the wind carried the faint, bitter scent of wet earth and loss.

A week later the Yuliana hospital held a farewell party for Doctor Charles. Wei moved among the guests with a calm, composed smile, the lantern light casting long shadows across the hospital lawn. Every threat had been silenced. Most important of all, he had sunk Sophia before she could truly escape him.

As soft music drifted on the warm night air and champagne glasses clinked in the distance, a vivid image unfolded in his mind. He saw Ayu on her knees in the quiet nursery, her small mouth working with quiet obedience, while he claimed Sophia from behind in their marriage bed—slow, deep, and unrelenting. The heightened sensitivity of his new weapon allowed him to feel every reluctant clench, every tremor of her body as she struggled between shame and unwanted pleasure. The fantasy settled heavy and warm inside him, a private, burning satisfaction that no one else could touch.

The music played on, but Wei barely heard it.
