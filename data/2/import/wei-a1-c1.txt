# Act 1

## Chapter 1 = Sophia

### Beat 1

In the thick, wet heat of Semarang, 1952, Dr. Sophia Chang moved between iron beds in the low-roofed clinic as though carrying cool water to fevered skin. Sweat beaded along her pale collarbone and darkened the cotton of her blouse in soft crescents beneath her arms, yet her hands stayed steady—cool even—when they pressed a cloth to a child's burning forehead or stitched torn flesh with small, precise bites of the needle. Patients who could not afford the Dutch hospital came to her anyway, murmuring "Nona Sophia" or "Cici Dokter" with something close to reverence. Her fair skin, a gift from the Dutch half of her blood, glowed faintly under the kerosene lamps; locals called her Bule behind cupped hands, half in wonder, half in shy affection. She answered in gentle Javanese, her Hakka accent threading through like a silver string, and when Dutch words slipped out they sounded almost like apologies. Each day she poured herself into the work until her shoulders ached and her eyes stung with tiredness; each evening she knelt in the small stone church or carried baskets of rice and medicine to the kampung lanes, her mercy as natural and tireless as rain.

Three years into marriage, Sophia still caught her breath when Wei stepped through the doorway at dusk. He would shrug off his linen jacket, roll his sleeves, and crouch beside the rattan cradle where little Peter kicked fat legs and gurgled. The baby's laughter rolled through the house like bright coins, and Wei's own laugh—low, easy—joined it. Later, after the jongos had cleared the dinner plates and the last of the house lights dimmed, he turned to her in their wide teak bed. His fingers traced the damp curve of her spine, lifted strands of hair from her neck where the skin was warm and salty. He kissed the hollow of her throat, murmured against her ear in the dark mixture of languages they shared, and moved with a slow, deliberate hunger that made her feel seen, chosen, singular. Every night the same quiet certainty settled over her: this was the man she had married, the one who still wanted her with the same sharp need as the first time. Nothing had dulled.

### Beat 2

Zi Din Wei carried the memory of Surabaya like a stone in his pocket—smooth from years of turning it over, heavy all the same. His father's godowns had stood along the Kali Mas; as a boy he had run between sacks of sugar and bolts of cloth while Chinese and Javanese voices braided the air. Then came the Bersiap. He had seen neighbors—people who had shared rice from the same plate—dragged into the street, beaten, gone. Indo families like Sophia's vanished in the night. The terror stayed in him, a low current beneath his easy smile. Now, when a worker moved too slowly or answered too softly, the old words sometimes slipped out: sharp Javanese slang, a dismissive flick of the hand. Sophia heard them and corrected him quietly—never in front of others, never with anger. "They are people, Koko," she would say, touching his wrist. He nodded, jaw tight, and tried. But late at night, when the compound gates creaked and the wind carried distant gamelan, she felt the tension in his shoulders and understood: the armor had saved him once. He wore it still.

Peter Chang—Papa to his daughter and son-in-law, Tuan Besar to the babu who swept the verandas—had sailed the family fortune to Australian harbors during the Bersiap exodus, evading the mobs, but his letters still arrived like small, precise storms. Wei's job was rebuilding after the chaos, navigating republican quotas with workers still wary of old grudges from the revolution. The sugar mills must turn profit; the books must balance; the name must endure. Sophia had grown up watching ledgers devour evenings, watching her mother's shoulders curve under the same invisible weight. She hated it—the smell of molasses that clung to everything, the way numbers seemed to own people. When Wei offered to step in, ambitious and quick with figures, she felt the air lighten around her. The mills ran smoothly under his hand. She could study late, sit examinations, open the clinic doors each morning without guilt pressing on her chest. In return she asked few questions about the late nights, the sudden trips to distant plantations, the dust on his shoes that smelled faintly of cane and something sharper. To doubt him now would feel like spitting on the freedom he had bought her. Better to trust. Better to be grateful.

In the long sheds behind the factory, or in the shadowed rooms of the outer compound, Tuan Muda Wei offered certain women a different kind of mercy. A widowed seamstress became a clerk in the office; a young mother with hungry children found steady work sweeping the big house. An envelope appeared on a kitchen table once a month—enough for rice amid the rising prices, medicine from black-market stocks, school fees. Whispers moved among the babu: Tuan Wei was baik hati, generous beyond reason. Some lowered their eyes when they said it; others let a small, careful smile show. None spoke louder. He chose carefully—women who needed the money too badly to risk losing it, women whose silence he could count on. Each afternoon he returned to the main house smelling faintly of cane smoke and sweat. Each evening he bathed, changed into fresh linen, lifted little Peter onto his shoulders, and later drew Sophia against him until her breathing matched his. In the lamplight her fingers traced the familiar lines of his back, and she felt only the heat of being wanted, the quiet certainty of being enough. The other truths stayed outside the bedroom door, locked in the dark with the rest of the night.
