### Beat 21

The year was 1945. In Surabaya the Chinese community walked a razor’s edge. Some young men had joined the fight for independence—enlisting with local militias or serving in the Chinese Red Cross. A few wealthy Peranakan families, long entwined with the Dutch colonial world, were whispered to be sympathizers or informers. Most, though, simply tried to stay alive. They barred their doors, closed their shops, and waited out the looting and the stray shells.

Wei, not yet seventeen, had climbed the godown roof one afternoon and watched British naval guns tear into his father’s warehouses. For weeks the riverside stood still—no loading, no unloading. Many godowns along the Kalimas River had been abandoned as owners fled the city. Not the Zi family. They could not run. Their name had been tied to these waters for generations; their trade was stitched quietly into the life of the river and the people who used it. So Zi Din Hauw and his son stayed, guarding what remained.

Their warehouse, unfortunately, sat too near the cluster of old buildings the British had turned into a temporary command post. That placed it squarely in range of the HMS Sussex’s guns.

That October night Wei spotted them first: two low shapes slipping along the perimeter wall, each hugging a canvas sack. The sacks clinked softly—morphine ampoules, rolls of gauze, sulfa packets scavenged from an overturned British supply truck in the first days of chaos. A rifle cracked. One figure dropped. The other—the girl—ran straight toward the Zi godown, tears carving pale tracks through the soot on her cheeks.

Zi Din Hauw appeared in the narrow side doorway, face half-lit by distant fires. He watched them a long beat, then stepped aside. “Masuk. Cepat.” The air inside was heavy—molasses, dust, faint metallic rust from the tin roof. “Mlebu. Nanti mereka lihat.”

She introduced herself later as Martina. Not Javanese. Moluccan. Slim and strongly built, dark curls matted with sweat and ash, skin a deep warm mahogany that seemed to hold its own quiet strength even under torn clothing. Wide hips moved with a natural roll even when she hurried. One sleeve was dark with blood from a shallow graze on her upper arm.

Zi Din Hauw told his wife and Wei to look after her. His wife paused—many Moluccans had served in the KNIL and worked alongside the Dutch; to some they were automatically suspect. But both father and son saw only someone caught by the war, bleeding from it. A victim.

He guided her past the weighing scales into the rear storage room. Tall stacks of sugar sacks leaned unevenly; bolts of kain panjang and plain cotton made soft walls and corners. Then he nodded at Wei. “You tend to her.”

Wei had never spent unbroken time alone with anyone beyond his own blood, let alone a woman like Martina—a pejuang who had charged toward gunfire while he still counted sacks and tallied figures. His palms stayed damp, his throat stayed narrow. He felt small, reduced to nothing more than a godown boy.

Martina was a trained nurse. That explained the desperate run through open ground for those canvas sacks—morphine to dull agony, sulfa to stop infection from turning small wounds deadly, gauze to hold torn flesh together. She handled each item with the calm certainty of someone who had already seen what happened when they ran out.

Between the rolling booms of distant artillery they talked. Not about terror, but about the odd fire war kindled in people forced to carry it forward. Wei mostly listened, head down, fingers careful as he cleaned her graze. Sometimes Din Hauw appeared in the doorway, arms folded, saying nothing, simply watching how his son worked.

On the second night she told him her story. Her family had crewed the MV Sinar Bahagia. Heading toward Sydney, the ship was hit by Japanese fire—raided, burned. She survived the water and the flames, washed up eventually in Surabaya, and joined the pejuang to keep breathing.

The name “Sinar Bahagia” changed the air. Din Hauw, standing just inside the doorway, spoke quietly to Wei. That was the ship that had carried Aunt Sui Ing and her husband two years before.

The words struck Wei like wet cement settling in his chest. Breath came short. Grief built in slow, heavy courses. He had loved his aunt in the distant way children love elders, but never close enough to truly know her. Now the knowing came only as absence.

Martina watched the boy’s face crumple. She spoke more slowly, the Maluku rhythm softening her words. “Tapi Tuan Muda… itu kan sudah dua tahun lalu, ya?” One shoulder lifted in a small shrug. The motion drew the torn cloth tight across her chest.

Night thickened around them.

The lantern’s weak flame caught Martina’s skin, turning deep mahogany glossy with held heat. Sweat followed slow, deliberate paths—along the collarbone, down the smooth curve of her throat, pooling in the soft hollow under her raised arm. Wei knelt again to change the dressing, boiled cloth shaking faintly in his grip. The scent lifted—warm, unwashed, living: salt and skin and the deep, animal musk of days in hiding, sharpened by dried blood and the godown’s cloying sweetness of old sugar. His breath snagged. He had never been near enough to any woman to smell her like this, unguarded and close.

Outside the war drummed on—shells, scattered shouts, the ragged chant of arek-arek Surabaya calling “Merdeka!” Inside, another current moved.

On the third night she met his eyes and held them. Slowly she raised both arms. The shadowed space beneath opened—fine dark hair damp, curling, skin gleaming wet. A rich, earthy wave rolled out, heavy and unfiltered, flooding his lungs until the far-off explosions felt miles away.

“China… kamu suka lihat ketiak hitam kah?” she asked, voice pitched low.

Wei made no sound. His body answered—hard, insistent, more immediate than any street slogan.

“Awas! Nanti Papa tahu. Dia marah.” she whispered. Yet her hand guided his face nearer, letting him press close, breathe her in. The world shrank to heat, musk, and the quiet allowance she offered.

By the fourth day the graze was closing, the sulfa and clean bandaging doing their work. Wei understood it meant goodbye. But then the city shifted again.

On the banks of the Kalimas River, almost in sight of the Zi godown, near the old Internatio Building, Brigadier General A.W.S. Mallaby was killed in a sudden firefight. The British erupted in fury. Din Hauw turned to Martina that same evening. “Jangan pergi dulu.” Stay. The streets were no longer safe.

The news lit something bright and selfish in Wei’s chest. More time. He wanted more.

“Iya… sudah Tuhan punya mau, tina disini sama Wei.” she murmured. She drew him back into the shadowed corner among the sugar sacks. She lifted her arm again, offering the damp hollow. Wei found her nipple through the thin cloth—hard, peaked—rubbing slow circles, then lowering his mouth to suck gently. She exhaled a soft sound.

Her hand moved lower, found the rigid shape straining against his trousers. She stroked once, twice, then sank to her knees. Her lips closed around him, warm and deliberate. Wei’s fingers tangled in her matted curls, breath ragged.

After, she lay back on the folded kain panjang, legs parting in invitation. He settled between them, entered her slowly. They moved together—quiet moans, low grunts—while British patrols passed outside, boots heavy on the river path, voices sharp with anger.

The Mallaby killing sparked the British ultimatum. November 10 loomed.

Wei ran through the godown that morning to tell her, heart pounding with the news. But the corner was empty. The blanket folded, the small pile of scavenged supplies gone. She had slipped away in the dark hours.

By mid-morning the Zi family abandoned the godown. Surabaya was no longer a place to endure. They left what they could not carry, locked the doors for the last time, and joined the slow river of people fleeing the coming fire.

### Beat 23

He took them against stacked crates, on folded kain panjang, tasting the honest salt of their labor, the asem bloom of keringat. He never forced. Need and power did the work.

The peasant girls from the kampung edges were different. Barefoot, kain dusty, they trembled when he called them by name—not from fear, but from the strangeness of being wanted by the Tuan Muda. He liked the raw musk when he pressed his face to their ketiak becek, the broken sounds they made. After he finished he gave them money, kain, sometimes work in the house. They left flushed, eyes lowered, never speaking of it again.

Sophia never knew. She came home to the man who made her scream his name, who held her until sleep took her, who listened to her clinic stories with steady eyes. She believed him.

Linda’s meetings continued. Once, sometimes twice a month. When Sophia’s shifts grew longer, Wei drove to Ontrouw. Room 27. Slatted blinds, slow fan, sheets that smelled of frangipani and kretek. Linda waited, blouse half-unbuttoned. They moved together like memory—her breath catching when he licked the damp hollow under her arm, her full lips closing around him until he groaned her name. Afterward she smoked, watching him dress.

They never spoke of Sophia. Never spoke of ending it. Each time felt like the last, yet the next letter arrived.

By the end of 1951 Linda told him.

They met one last time at Ontrouw. Afternoon light slanted through the blinds, striping the bed in gold bars. She sat on the edge, kretek unlit between her fingers.

“I’m going back to Bandung,” she said. “My father’s estate needs me. The rubber trees aren’t going to tap themselves.”

“When?”

“Next month. The train leaves the 15th.”

He reached for her hand. She let him take it, but her fingers stayed loose.

“Don’t come to the station,” she said. “Don’t make it harder.”

He looked at her—the sharp cheekbones softer now, the mouth set in a small, steady line.

“I never wanted to lose you,” he said, voice rough.

Linda smiled—small, sad. “You didn’t lose me, Awei. You chose. And that’s all right.”

She leaned in, kissed him once—slow, deliberate. When she pulled back her eyes were dry.

“Be kind to her,” she whispered. “She’s the one who stays.”

She dressed without hurry. At the door she paused.

“Don’t follow me down the stairs. Let me go alone.”

He stayed seated. The door clicked shut. Her footsteps faded. The room held her scent a little longer—kretek, jasmine, warm skin—then the fan stirred it away.

Wei sat until the light bars lengthened. When he finally stood the room felt emptier. He left the key on the dresser and drove home through the late-afternoon traffic.

Sophia was in the sitting room when he arrived. She sat folding medical notes, humming a Hakka lullaby to the quiet air. 

“You’re late,” she said softly.

“Traffic,” he answered.

He crossed to her, kissed her forehead, sat beside her. Sophia’s hand rested light on his knee. He felt the steady warmth of it, the quiet certainty that had always been there.
