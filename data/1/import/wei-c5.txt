### Beat 17

The road home lay too quiet. Halfway between city lights and the house the front right tire burst with a sharp, wet pop. The car jerked hard right. Wei cursed low, hands tight on the wheel, fighting it until gravel crunched under the tires and he coasted to the shoulder.

He stepped out. Flat. Dead flat. Trunk open—no spare. He had never once checked.

Night pressed in close. Paddy fields stretched black on either side, frogs pulsing loud in the dark, no house lights, no passing cars. Just stars beginning to prick through the haze and the whine of mosquitoes.

Headlights flickered far down the road. A single bicycle, slow, wobbling. An older man—Pak Rahmat, Wei placed him after a moment, from village meetings—pedaled closer, squinting into the beam of his own weak lamp.

“Tuan Muda?” the man called, surprised. “Ada apa?”

Wei lifted a hand. “Ban kempes. Tidak ada ban serep.”

Pak Rahmat leaned the bicycle against the car fender. “Tunggu sebentar. Saya panggil orang.”

He pedaled off into the black. Wei leaned against the warm hood, arms folded, waiting. Mosquitoes sang. Stars multiplied overhead.

Twenty minutes later real headlights appeared—motorcycle with sidecar. Pak Rahmat rode pillion behind a younger man loaded with tools. The technician gave a short respectful nod, set to work without chatter. Jack up, lug wrench clinking, spare tire appearing from somewhere—Wei did not ask where.

While the man labored, Pak Rahmat pointed toward a faint glow in the distance. “Rumah saya dekat, Tuan. Mending tunggu di dalam. Minum teh dulu.”

Wei considered only a heartbeat. “Baiklah.”

The house stood small and wooden, raised on stilts. Lantern light spilled soft from the open doorway. A young woman waited there—twenty-five, perhaps—hair wrapped simply in kain, eyes widening when she recognized the man stepping up behind Pak Rahmat. Nurani. Wei only smiled faintly, gaze sliding back toward the doorway. He already pictured Nurani inside his room—petite body stretched out, long legs wrapped around him, full lips gasping.

Outside, the technician called softly—the tire was done.

Wei stood. “Saya pamit dulu.” He pressed money into Pak Rahmat’s palm. “Untuk bayar tukang.”

Wei stepped into the night. Pak Rahmat walked him to the car. The technician wiped his hands, ready to go.

As Wei slid behind the wheel he glanced once more toward the lit doorway.

Nurani stood framed there, small silhouette against the warm lantern glow—hip cocked, one long leg bent slightly, blouse clinging, eyes still locked on him with that same teasing spark.

He started the engine. The car rolled away smoothly, tires humming over the dark road.

In the rearview mirror the house shrank, then disappeared.

But the weight of her name—and the slow burn of her flirtatious, leggy, petite tease—followed him all the way home.  

### Beat 18

Sophia waited at the long teak table, candles already lit, plates covered to keep the food warm. The house sat quiet except for the soft tick of the ceiling fan and the distant call of night insects beyond the screened verandah. She wore a simple cotton dress, hair loose, one bare foot tucked beneath her on the chair. When Wei finally stepped through the doorway, shirt damp at the small of his back from the drive, she looked up with a small, relieved smile.

“You’re late,” she said, rising to meet him.

“Flat tire. Halfway home. No spare.” He kissed her forehead, lingering a second. “Someone from the village helped. Nice old man. Sent a technician out of nowhere.”

She nodded, brushing a speck of dust from his collar. “You must be starving.”

They sat. She uncovered the dishes—steamed fish, sambal, rice still fragrant. Wei told her the rest while they ate: the dark road, the frogs, the small house on stilts, the tea that tasted of woodsmoke. He left out Nurani entirely. Sophia listened without interrupting, fork moving slowly.

She didn’t ask about Linda. Didn’t mention Rita. Instead she reached across the table and touched his wrist. “Thank you for choosing Ayu and Wulan. They’ve been so helpful already. The house feels… lighter with them here.”

Wei gave a small nod, pleased. “Good babu are hard to find.”

Sophia set her fork down. “I have news too.” She drew a slow breath. “Semarang General Hospital offered me a new duty. Better facilities, more cases—real impact. I’ll be leaving the church clinic.”

Wei’s eyebrows lifted. Pride flickered across his face, quick and genuine. “That’s big, Cici. I’m glad. You deserve it.”

She smiled, but the smile carried a shadow. “It means longer hours. Night shifts sometimes. I’ll practically live there. Barely home.”

He studied her a moment. Reached for her hand. “If you must… then it’s okay.”

She squeezed his fingers. “There’s more. A letter came from Papa. He’ll be in Semarang next month. He mentioned a new mill south of the city—almost toward Salatiga. Wants to talk about it.”

Wei leaned back, considering. “I remember the site. I’ll have Bagas check the papers tomorrow. Make sure everything lines up.”

They finished the meal in comfortable quiet. After the plates were cleared, Sophia leaned against the back of her chair, wineglass cradled in both hands, studying him with that familiar half-smile.

“You know,” she said, voice dropping playfully, “I keep thinking about what Linda said.”

Wei groaned softly, already knowing. “Don’t start.”

She laughed—light, bright. “She’s right, though. You’re still… intact. After all these years.”

He flushed, shifting in his seat. “Jesus, Cici.”

Sophia tilted her head, eyes sparkling. “It’s healthier, you know. Cleaner. And—honestly—sexier. Like the Jewish men. Smooth. No extra skin catching anything.”

In the shadowed doorway to the kitchen passage, Mbok Yati had stood motionless the whole time, tray still balanced on one palm, forgotten. She had come to clear the last dishes, but the conversation held her in place. The words—intact, knife, frenulum, inner walls—drifted to her like smoke. She knew those words from long-ago nights when Tuan Besar Peter had spoken them to Nona Sophia’s mother in hushed tones, in rooms she was meant to clean but never interrupt. The same teasing promise. The same man’s fear masked as laughter.

She did not move until the candles were snuffed and the house settled. Only then did she pad silently back to the servants’ wing, bare feet whispering on tiles.

### Beat 19

Hours later the house lay dark and still. Sophia slept deeply beside him, breath even, one arm flung across the pillow. Wei stared at the ceiling for a long time. The image she painted refused to fade. Heat lingered low in his belly, restless.

Quietly he eased out of bed. Bare feet on cool tiles. He moved through the shadowed corridor, past closed doors, until he reached the small room at the end.

Ayu’s door stood slightly ajar. A faint line of moonlight slipped through the gap.

He paused, hand on the frame. Inside, two shapes lay tangled on the narrow bed: Ayu and Wulan, sleeping close, limbs loosely entwined, sarongs rumpled from the heat. Their breathing rose and fell in soft unison.

Wei pushed the door wider. The hinges gave the smallest creak.

Both girls stirred. Ayu opened her eyes first—wide, startled—then Wulan beside her, blinking sleep away, cheeks already flushing in the dim light.

“Tuan…” Ayu whispered, sitting up slowly, clutching the edge of the kain to her chest.

Wei stepped inside, closing the door behind him with a quiet click. Moonlight painted silver stripes across the floorboards.

“I couldn’t sleep,” he said, voice low. “Come here. Both of you. Malam ini, kita main bareng yuk.”

They hesitated. Ayu’s gaze darted to Wulan, then back to him. Shame colored their faces—dark, hot—but neither moved to cover more. Wulan bit her lower lip, eyes dropping to the mat.

Now she listened to the familiar cadence: the creak of the bed, the hushed commands, the rising and falling breaths. She had watched since Sophia was small—had seen Peter Chang, the Tuan Besar himself, in this very room years ago, door cracked just like this, moonlight spilling the same way. Then, without a sound, she turned and padded back down the corridor, bare feet silent on the tiles, carrying the night’s knowledge—and the promise of future usefulness—with her into the house’s deeper shadows.
