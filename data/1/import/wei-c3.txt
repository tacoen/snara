# Act 1

## Chapter 3 - Moeramanis

### Beat 9

Wei stood near the hibiscus hedge, the road still empty under the late afternoon sun. Dust hung motionless in the air; no rumble of the church bus yet. The clock in his head ticked louder with every passing minute. In his palm lingered the faint warmth of the parcel he had just pressed into Ayu's hands—a Peter Pan-collared dress, pale cotton printed with tiny embroidered flowers, far finer than any servant's wage could buy.

Ayu had accepted it with both hands, dark eyes shining. "Terima kasih, Tuan Muda Wei," she whispered. Then, quick as a mischief, she rose on her toes and kissed him—brief, possessive, lips brushing the corner of his mouth before she slipped away toward the outdoor bathhouse.

Wei turned and walked through the clotheslines strung behind the kitchen. Wulan was there, reaching high to unpin damp sheets. The motion stretched her blouse tight across her chest; beneath one lifted arm the cotton clung dark and damp, the shadowed hollow glistening with the day's honest labor. Fine dark hair curled there, catching faint light.

He moved between the lines without sound, stepping close behind her. His arms circled her waist; his face pressed to the salty nape of her neck. He inhaled slow—the thick, warm musk of sun-heated skin, pheromones rising sharp and real after hours under the open sky.

Wulan stiffened for half a heartbeat—she had seen the earlier exchange with Ayu—then softened against him, shoulders easing.

"Jangan, Wan," she murmured, voice pitched low, half protest.

"Ya... sayang ya," he answered against her skin, breath warm. "My wife arriving soon." Then softer, teasing: "Kemarin kita belum selesai, Lan."

He stole one quick kiss—lips grazing the smooth curve where neck met shoulder—then stepped back. Wulan nodded once, shy, eyes lowered. "Besok, Wan."

Wei tweaked the tip of her nose lightly and continued into the kitchen. "Mbok Yati! Pisang goreng and sweet tea, sekarang! Nyonya will be tired when she comes."

From the bathing area came the sudden hiss of the shower—slow, deliberate streams striking tile. He pictured Ayu there: languid tilt of shoulders under the water, soap tracing lazy paths down warm skin, deliberate slowness in every movement. So small, so knowing. The salt-and-musk promise coiled low in his gut again. "Damn," he hissed under his breath, "she stays in my head."

He slipped outside behind the kitchen, eyes fixed on the bamboo screen that shielded the bathhouse. Crouching low, he peered through the narrow gaps between the slats.

Ayu moved slow inside—graceful lift of one arm to rinse soap from her shoulder, the delicate curve of limb exposing the shadowed hollow beneath. Water gleamed on her skin; faint lather clung in soft bubbles; the dark curl of hair there caught stray light like a hidden secret. She turned slightly, aware perhaps of being watched, letting the motion linger.

Wei did not hear the heavy rumble of the church bus rolling through the compound gates.

Sophia stepped down first, shoulders rounded from the long ride, followed by Linda and Linda's niece Rita. Sophia lifted her collar, sniffed, and groaned softly. "I need a bath. I smell like animal."

Linda laughed, light. "Cute Dutch animal, maybe."

Rita, already wandering ahead, eyes wide at the whitewashed sprawl of the house and the manicured garden, drifted deeper into the grounds while the two women moved indoors. Sophia waved her on without turning. "Explore a little, sayang. We'll be inside."

Rita walked farther, sandals scuffing soft on the path. There—half-hidden by banana leaves and the hibiscus—Wei crouched near the bathhouse, body angled toward the screen, shoulders tense with focus.

When he sensed her he startled hard, face draining pale. He scrambled upright, planting himself between her and the bamboo in one quick motion.

"Who are you?" he demanded, voice sharper than he meant.

Rita tilted her head, curious rather than frightened. "Kamu siapa? Ada apa di sini?"

Wei squared his shoulders, hand dropping to his hip. "Young lady, I am the master of this house. Who the hell are you?"

Ayu emerged then from the bathhouse, towel-drying her hair, skin still glowing with water and warmth. She caught the edge of the exchange and dipped her head at once. "Ini Tuan Muda Wei. Kamu siapa?"

"Rita Moeramanis."

"Moeramanis?" Wei's brow creased. "Linda Moeramanis?"

Rita nodded once.

"My wife is back?" Panic threaded thin through his voice.

"Yeah. Sorry, I was just walking. They already went inside—look tired."

Wei spun without another word and hurried toward the main house, footsteps quick on the gravel path.

Rita stood beside Ayu a moment longer. She glanced at the younger woman—same slight build, same quiet prettiness, same age almost. "Kamu babu?"

Ayu nodded quickly, eyes lowered in the automatic deference that marked the space between their worlds.

"Dia siapa?" Rita asked, softer.

"Tuan Wei. Saya baru di sini... pengasuh bayi."

"Oh." Rita studied her again—the new dress, pale cotton with tiny flowers, still crisp against Ayu's damp skin. "Baju baru?"

Ayu's fingers brushed the collar once. "Iya... Tuan Muda kasih tadi."

Rita gave a single nod, small and sharp. She turned toward the house, following the path Wei had taken. Behind her Ayu hurried toward the kitchen, towel still clutched in one hand.

A chill traced Rita's spine. Ayu could have been her mirror—same height, same delicate frame. And the man who had just been staring through the screen at her like she was something to devour was hurrying back to greet his saintly wife.

### Beat 10

The parlor smelled of cooled afternoon air and the faint sweetness of frying oil drifting from the kitchen. Sophia sat in the deep rattan armchair nearest the window, shoes kicked off, bare feet tucked beneath her. The long ride from Girisonta had left faint lines of tiredness around her eyes, but she still smiled when Wei leaned down to kiss her forehead.

Ayu entered then, balancing a wide bamboo tray. Steam rose from the golden pisang goreng piled high; beside them small glasses of sweet tea sweated beads onto the wood. She kept her head lowered, steps measured and careful across the black-and-white tiles. The new dress—pale cotton with tiny embroidered flowers—swung softly against her legs.

"Cici, here is Ayu," Wei said, voice smooth as polished teak. He gestured toward the girl as though the afternoon in the garden had never happened. "The new babysitter I selected for you. The best I could find." He turned his head toward the back hall. "Wulan! Mbok Yati, mana Wulan?"

Wulan appeared a moment later from the laundry room doorway, blouse sleeves rolled to the elbow, a faint damp spot still darkening the fabric beneath one arm. She stood straight, hands clasped in front, expression plain and composed.

"Wulan will handle the laundry and help Mbok Yati," Wei continued. "These two girls will keep the house running while you are at the clinic, Sophia."

Sophia reached out and touched Ayu's wrist lightly as the girl set the tray on the low table. "Wei, thank you. You are so thoughtful. The baby's laundry has been a nightmare lately."

Linda let out a short, bright laugh from the sofa. "Careful, Sophia. Little Peter probably makes a mess just like his father used to. I remember Wei in college—he always needed someone to clean up after him."

She gave Wei a pointed look, one brow arched in familiar teasing. Sophia shrugged it off with a tired smile, already reaching for a piece of fried banana. Rita sat on the low stool near the wall, knees drawn up at first, watching the room like someone learning the rules of a game she had not been taught.

When Ayu bent to place the last glass, Rita caught it: a quick, stolen glance between Wei and the babysitter. Nothing overt—just the briefest lift of his eyes to hers, a flicker of something private passing between them before both looked away.

Rita's stomach tightened. The new dress. The careful title of "babysitter." The way Wei had crouched outside the bathhouse earlier, body rigid with focus. Then she saw him step close to Wulan as the older girl turned to leave—his hand landing once, light and playful, on the curve of her backside. Wulan did not flinch; she only dipped her head a fraction and continued toward the kitchen. Rita exhaled, only then realizing she had been holding her breath.

She took a bite of pisang goreng. The sweetness coated her tongue, then turned thick, almost cloying.

Wei settled into the chair beside Sophia, one arm draped casually along the back. "So, you planning to stay till tomorrow here?"

Sophia nodded, already reaching for her tea. "Like always, Wei! This Moeramanis is dying for Mbok Yati's sambal."

Wei laughed—low, easy. Linda laughed too, leaning forward so her shoulder brushed his for a moment.

Rita watched the exchange. Another glance passed between her aunt and Wei—longer this time, eyes holding just a beat beyond polite. Something unspoken hung in the air, thick as the humid dusk settling outside the open windows.

She looked away, toward the kitchen doorway where Wulan had disappeared. Mbok Yati's voice carried faintly, scolding gently about something. Not her, Rita thought. Obviously not her.

She picked up another piece of fried banana, broke it in half, let the steam rise against her fingers. Wei was not the perfect husband Sophia had always described in letters. Something felt off—fishy, the word surfaced in her mind like a bad smell.

Then she felt it: his gaze on her.

She had shifted on the stool without thinking, legs parting slightly as she leaned back against the wall. The short skirt of her travel dress rode up; the edge of her cotton panties—still faintly dusty from the bus ride—showed in the gap between her thighs.

Wei's eyes locked there. Steady. Unhurried.

Rita clamped her legs shut fast, heat rushing to her face. Shame prickled along her skin. She tugged the hem down, crossed her ankles tight.

Wei smiled—small, almost gentle—and turned back to the conversation as though nothing had happened. "So... that was your niece?" he asked Linda, nodding toward Rita.

Linda leaned closer, voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper—explaining Rita's age, her family, perhaps something more.

Rita sat very still, the half-eaten pisang goreng forgotten in her lap. The parlor light caught the fine sheen of sweat along Wei's collar; the same light made the embroidered flowers on Ayu's new dress glow softly as the girl moved quietly back toward the hall.

Outside, the cicadas started their evening chorus. Inside, the conversation flowed on—easy, familiar, laced with jokes no one fully explained. Rita listened to the surface of it all, but her mind stayed on the glances, the touches, the way Wei's eyes had lingered without apology.

She pressed her knees tighter together, the cotton of her dress suddenly too thin, and stared at the cooling tea in her glass, the sweetness in her mouth now tasting like ash.

### Beat 11

Linda and Rita stayed the night. The promise of Mbok Yati's sambal—the sharp, fermented heat that always tasted like home to the Moeramanis women—had been enough. At dinner Little Peter held court in Sophia's arms, tiny fists waving, drawing soft coos from every corner of the table. The baby gurgled and kicked; the room filled with easy laughter. Mbok Yati moved around them in quiet pride, setting down each dish with perfect symmetry: rice steaming white, sambal glistening crimson in its shallow bowl, the table laid as though nothing in the house had ever been out of place.

Linda told the room Rita was taking piano lessons and asked her to play. The girl sat at the grand piano in the parlor, fingers steady despite the day's weight. A bright Chopin nocturne spilled out—clean, precise, filling the space with something lighter than the humid night outside. Sophia cradled the baby against her shoulder, swaying gently; Wei refilled glasses with practiced charm, the perfect host. Laughter rose and fell like waves. By ten the house quieted. Linda, worn from the retreat and the wine, slipped into deep sleep in the guest room. Everyone else followed.

Rita could not sleep. The day's unease clung to her like damp cloth against skin. She tried closing her eyes, but the house pressed back—too still, too full of things unsaid. She rose barefoot, nightdress whispering against her legs, and padded down the dim corridor toward the small bathroom near the laundry. Cold cream, rosewater—the small rituals that steadied her.

The bathroom door stood ajar. Lamplight spilled gold across the tiles in a narrow stripe. She paused.

Wei stood inside, back to the doorway, shoulders relaxed in the way of a man who believed the house belonged to him alone. One by one he lifted damp garments from the wicker hamper: Sophia's blouse, still carrying the faint lily of her perfume; Ayu's new dress, soap-scented and crisp; then he paused. His fingers closed around a scrap of faded cotton.

Rita's panties.

He lifted them slowly, almost reverently, to his face. The fabric bunched against his nose; he inhaled long and deep, eyes half-closed, savoring the intimate musk that had gathered there through the long bus ride, the garden heat, the day's honest salt and sweat. A low sound escaped his throat—not quite a groan, more a private sigh of possession. He held the breath in his lungs as though it were something rare, something stolen and therefore his.

Rita's foot caught the edge of a low pedestal vase. It tipped.

"Prang!!"

Porcelain shattered across the tiles in a bright, violent cascade. Wei's head snapped up. The vase was Chinese antique—Peter Chang's favorite; Sophia's father would never forgive the loss. "Damn!" he shouted.

The panties dropped from his fingers back into the pile. For one heartbeat their eyes locked—his wide with shock, then narrowing into something darker, hungrier; hers frozen, pulse hammering in her throat.

"Rita?" he said.

He straightened slowly from the shattered porcelain, eyes never leaving hers. "Couldn't sleep, piano girl?"

Rita swallowed, throat dry as paper. "I... I was just going to the bathroom. For my skin-care routine. Cold cream. Rosewater. That’s all."

A single bead of sweat gathered at her hairline, trembled, then slid—slow, deliberate—down the curve of her forehead, tracing the delicate line of her temple, slipping behind her ear, and finally disappearing beneath the collar of her thin nightdress toward the hollow of her neck. Wei watched its path the entire way. Then he smiled—small, knowing, the corners of his mouth curling like secrets.

"That vase was Ta Laupan's favorite." He pointed at the fragments. Rita looked down, unable to answer. She pictured him as all too perverse—a man who had already claimed almost every woman in this house, and now she stood alone with him near midnight, the rest of the world asleep. She trembled. "So-sorry."

Wei slipped something small into his pocket—the panties, folded quick and neat. "Well... we should replace that vase before Sophia realizes it."

Rita nodded fast, eyes pleading. The stakes were too high. Linda would never forgive her; Peter Chang's possessions were sacred.

"I-I do anything," she begged. The word hung between them. Wei's eyes darkened, pupils swallowing the lamplight. The word "anything" shifted something in Wei.

"Well. You watched me," he said, striking without warning. "Peeking at my babu bathing this evening, yes?"

Rita shook her head fast, too fast. "No! I didn’t."

He stepped closer. The lie hung between them, thin as smoke.

"Hmm." A soft grunt of disbelief. Rita felt his breath near her shoulder. "Well... young lady." His hand settled on her shoulder—firm, warm through the cotton. "Your aunt Linda..."

He let the name linger, then dropped the hamper with a dull clatter. Fingers tightened just enough to guide her—steering, not dragging—down the shadowed corridor toward his study.

Inside, the door clicked shut behind them. Moonlight sliced through the slatted blinds, striping the polished floor and the grand piano that dominated the room.

"Linda said you are good at piano," he continued, voice dropping softer now, almost conversational. Rita had braced for something that would shame the Moeramanis name, but no—he guided her to the bench. She sat because his hand on her shoulder made sitting the only safe option.

"Play." Rita trembled—hands shaking above the keys.

Wei whispered close, "Play so everybody knows you are playing it."

She glanced at him, nervously, knowing it was not about the piano. Wei continued, "While I taste you."

The image flooded her: Wei in the garden, eyes hungry on Ayu's raised arm, the salt-glisten of skin and the dark curl of hair beneath; the soft spank he gave Wulan; Wei with Linda's wrist under his thumb at dinner; Wei inhaling her own soiled cotton like a man claiming territory; the lies he fed Sophia night after night. He was menace wrapped in silk. Master. Pervert. Husband.

"Play," he whispered, bending close. His mouth hovered near her ear—breath warm against the sensitive skin just behind the lobe, close enough that she felt the heat of his words brush the fine hairs there.

She shivered violently. Fingers found the keys anyway. Slow at first—halting, uncertain notes. Then the melody gathered: Debussy’s Arabesque No. 1 unfurled into the room, liquid and mournful, the notes rippling like water over stone.

Tears slipped down her cheeks. She sobbed once—quiet, choked.

Wei sat beside her on the bench. One hand lifted, thumb brushing the wet trail from her eye, wiping it away with surprising tenderness.

"Beautiful," he whispered, the word thick against her temple.

She stopped playing. The final notes of the Arabesque hung in the air—fragile, unfinished.

Wan Wei’s hands closed around her wrists. He lifted her arms upward, high beside her head, elbows bent, exposing the smooth hollows beneath. The motion stretched her nightdress taut across her chest; cool night air kissed the newly bared skin.

He leaned in close. Nose brushing the delicate curve of her armpit—soft, shadowed, still faintly damp from the day’s heat. He inhaled deeply, long and deliberate, drawing the salt of her skin, the warm musk that had gathered there after the long bus ride, the garden walk, the nervous sweat of the evening. A low, appreciative hum vibrated in his throat.

"Can you play that with one hand?" he murmured against the tender flesh.

Rita shook her head, small, frantic. "No..."

"Well... try." His voice dropped, velvet over steel. "Because you smell delicious."

He lingered there a moment longer—nose pressing into the hollow, lips grazing the fine dark hair—then pulled back abruptly. From his pocket he drew the scrap of faded cotton—her panties, still carrying the day’s intimate scent. He lifted them to his face and inhaled again, eyes half-lidded.

"You smell so authentic, Rita," he whispered, the words thick with hunger. "Real. Not like the perfumed ones."

He pocketed them once more. Leaned close again, breath hot against her ear.

"I will not tell anyone about the vase." A pause, the offer hanging like smoke. "If you play those notes... while I stay close."

The words slid into her like a spell—hypnotic, inevitable. Her pulse roared in her ears. She nodded once—small, trembling—and her fingers found the keys again. One hand this time, hesitant, picking out the melody’s opening phrases. Slow. Broken. Beautiful.

Wan Wei dropped low, sliding beneath the piano’s broad belly. Hands pushed up the hem of her nightdress—thin cotton whispering over thighs—guiding her legs apart with firm insistence. Warm breath ghosted over her skin—teasing—then he settled close, his presence overwhelming in the shadowed space beneath the instrument.

Rita’s free hand gripped the edge of the bench, knuckles white. A sob caught in her throat, half fear, half something darker. The Debussy notes fractured, stumbled, then steadied again beneath the insistent press of his nearness. The heat of the moment swallowed everything: the broken vase, the peeping in the garden, the secret Linda carried, Sophia sleeping upstairs with the baby. Only this remained—the trembling keys under her fingers, the low, greedy sound of his breathing from beneath the piano.

She played on.

When the melody finally dissolved into a single, sustained discord, her body shuddered once—sharp, helpless. Wan Wei eased back, rising slow, deliberate. He knew now—this niece wanted him. The trembling denial had burned away in the heat of the moment.

He cupped her cheek, thumb brushing the tear tracks still drying there. Voice gentle now, almost tender: "Want more?"

Rita looked up at him—eyes glassy, pupils blown wide with raw confusion and desire. The religious retreat of the day before, the hymns sung on the bus, the saintly quiet of Sophia’s world—all forgotten. She nodded once, small and certain.

Wan Wei smiled—slow, possessive. "Not now," he murmured. "Not while your aunt is here. Not while my wife is here." A pause, breath warm against her lips. "Not yet."

She nodded again, quicker this time.

"Fine."

He leaned in and kissed her—deep, claiming, tasting the salt of her tears mingled with the lingering warmth of the night. Lips bruised, tongues sliding once more in lazy echo of the closeness beneath the piano. Then he pulled back.

They parted. Rita slipped from the bench on unsteady legs, nightdress falling back into place, damp with nervous sweat between her thighs. Wan Wei watched her go—eyes tracing the curve of her retreating form—then turned to the shattered vase in the corridor, already calculating how the morning would begin.
